Our Story

Two Sisters, One Kitchen Table Dream

Manjit and Raj have called Central Texas home for decades. This is where they raised their families, celebrated their festivals, and built their lives. But even after all that time, one thing was hard to hold onto — the smell of turmeric on your hands, cardamom simmering in the chai, mustard seeds popping in hot oil. The little things that make a kitchen feel like your mother's kitchen.

For years, they made do — driving hours to a bigger city for the right spice, or waiting for family to bring things back in their suitcases. Then they thought: if it's this hard for us, after living here so long, it must be hard for our neighbors too.

So in 2015, they opened the doors to Om Spice & Sweets.

It's still a small store, run the same way it started — just two sisters, side by side, doing this because they love it. No big chain behind it, no corporate backing. Just family, taking care of family. We're working on making sure more of Central Texas knows we're here — Temple, Killeen, Fort Hood, Waco, Belton, and everywhere in between — because no one should have to drive further than they have to for something that tastes like home.

Step inside, and you'll likely find Manjit or Raj right there at the counter. They'll ask how your family's doing. They'll tell you which chili powder actually brings the heat and which one's just for color. If it's your first time, don't be surprised if one of them walks you down the aisle personally, chatting the whole way.

Because here, you're not just a customer. You're welcome at the table.

Cooking Tips

10 Essential Indian Spices Every Pantry Needs

From cumin to garam masala, here are the spices you'll reach for again and again.
  1. Turmeric (Haldi) — The golden spice. Anti-inflammatory, gives color to nearly every curry. A pinch goes a long way.
  2. Cumin (Jeera) — Earthy and warm. Used whole in tadka or ground into curries. One of the most versatile spices.
  3. Coriander (Dhania) — Citrusy and mild. Ground coriander is the base of most Indian gravies. Works with almost everything.
  4. Garam Masala — A warming blend of cardamom, cloves, cinnamon, and more. Always add at the end of cooking for best flavor.
  5. Red Chili Powder (Lal Mirch) — Heat levels vary by brand. Start small and adjust. Kashmiri chili gives color without too much heat.
  6. Mustard Seeds (Rai) — Essential for tadka. Drop them in hot oil and wait for the pop. Used in South Indian cooking daily.
  7. Asafoetida (Hing) — A pinch adds a deep savory note to dal and vegetable dishes. Also aids digestion.
  8. Cardamom (Elaichi) — Green cardamom for sweet and savory dishes. Adds a floral, aromatic lift to chai and biryani.
  9. Cloves (Laung) — Pungent and strong. Use whole in rice dishes and curries. A little goes a long way.
  10. Cinnamon (Dalchini) — Whole sticks in rice and curries add warmth without sweetness. Cassia variety is most common in Indian cooking.

All of these are in stock at Om Spice & Sweets. Stop by and we'll help you find what you need. — Raj always tells customers to start with cumin and coriander; everything else builds from there.

Ingredient Guide

A Beginner's Guide to Cooking with Dal & Lentils

Toor, chana, urad, moong — not sure which dal is which? Here's a quick breakdown.

Dal is a staple in every Indian kitchen. Here are the most common varieties and how to use them:

  • Toor Dal — Yellow, mild, cooks soft. Used in sambar and everyday dal. The most common dal in Indian homes.
  • Chana Dal — Split chickpeas, nutty flavor. Holds its shape when cooked. Great for dal fry and chilla (savory pancakes).
  • Urad Dal — Black lentils, creamy texture. The key ingredient in dal makhani and idli/dosa batter.
  • Moong Dal — Split green gram, light and easy to digest. Cooks quickly. Perfect when you want a simple, comforting dal.
  • Masoor Dal — Red lentils, cook fastest (15-20 min). Mild flavor, turns golden when cooked. Great for quick weeknight meals.

Tip: Always wash dal 2-3 times before cooking. No soaking needed for masoor and moong — toor, chana, and urad benefit from 30 min soaking. — Manjit says the key to great dal is patience: let it simmer low and slow.

Basmati, Sona Masoori & More: Choosing the Right Rice

Not all rice is the same. Here's which variety to use for what dish.
  • Basmati — Long grain, fragrant, fluffy. The go-to for biryani, pulao, and any special occasion dish. Aged basmati has the best aroma.
  • Sona Masoori — Medium grain, everyday rice in South Indian homes. Lighter than basmati, pairs well with sambar and rasam.
  • Ponni Rice — Short grain, slightly sticky. Ideal for idli and dosa batter. Also works as a daily cooking rice.
  • Parboiled Rice (Ukda Chawal) — Firm, separate grains. Holds up well in leftovers. Popular in Maharashtra and Gujarat for daily use.
  • Rosematta / Matta Rice — Red rice from Kerala. Nutty flavor, chewy texture. Traditionally eaten with fish curry or thoran.

Tip: For fluffy basmati, soak 20-30 min, then boil in plenty of salted water. Drain and let it rest 5 min before serving. — Raj's rule of thumb: one cup rice, two cups water, and don't peek until it's done.

Culture & Food

Traditional Indian Sweets to Try This Festival Season

Gulab jamun, jalebi, barfi, ladoo — the sweets that make every celebration special.
  • Gulab Jamun — Deep-fried milk solid balls soaked in rose-scented sugar syrup. Soft, sweet, and utterly addictive. The most popular Indian sweet across the world.
  • Jalebi — Crispy, crunchy swirls soaked in saffron syrup. Best eaten warm. The contrast between the crisp exterior and syrup-soaked inside is unmatched.
  • Barfi — A fudge-like sweet, usually made with condensed milk and flavored with cardamom. Common varieties include plain, coconut, and pistachio (pista barfi).
  • Ladoo — Round sweets made from various bases. Besan ladoo (gram flour), coconut ladoo, and motichoor ladoo (tiny boondi pearls) are crowd favorites.
  • Kaju Katli — Diamond-shaped cashew fudge. Smooth, melt-in-your-mouth texture. A premium sweet, often given as a gift during Diwali and weddings.

We stock fresh Indian sweets at Om Spice & Sweets. Come by and pick up a box for your next celebration. — Manjit's personal favorite: warm gulab jamun with a scoop of ice cream on top.

Shopping List

How to Stock Your Indian Pantry: A Complete Shopping List

Walk into any Indian grocery store with confidence. Here's exactly what to buy.

Building a well-stocked Indian pantry doesn't have to be overwhelming. Start with these basics:

  • Spices — Turmeric, cumin, coriander, garam masala, red chili powder, mustard seeds, asafoetida
  • Dals — Toor dal, chana dal, urad dal, moong dal, masoor dal (start with 2-3)
  • Rice — Basmati for special dishes, Sona Masoori or Ponni for everyday
  • Flour — Atta (whole wheat) for roti, besan (chickpea flour) for pakoras and chilla
  • Oil & Ghee — Mustard oil for cooking, ghee for finishing and sweets
  • Fresh — Onions, garlic, ginger, green chilies, curry leaves, cilantro
  • Canned & Packaged — Coconut milk, diced tomatoes, frozen peas, frozen parathas
  • Snacks & Extras — Papad, pickles, chai, chutneys, frozen samosas

Not sure where to start? Stop by the store and ask Manjit or Raj — they'll point you in the right direction. — Raj's top tip: always keep frozen parathas on hand for the nights you don't feel like cooking.
